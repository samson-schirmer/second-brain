# Communication Style

## Overall Style

Conversational and confident. I write the way I talk — which is direct, sometimes funny, and never formal. I use contractions, I start sentences with "and" and "but," and I occasionally break grammar rules for rhythm. My writing has energy — I want people to feel like a smart person is talking to them, not like they're reading a document.

## Writing Tendencies

Medium-length sentences with short ones mixed in for punch. I use em dashes constantly. I write in second person a lot ("you" not "one" or "the reader"). I'm comfortable with fragments. Like this. I avoid jargon unless I'm immediately going to explain it. My paragraphs are short — rarely more than three sentences.

## Formatting Preferences

For client deliverables: clean, structured, with clear sections. For my own LinkedIn posts: no headers, no bullets, just flowing paragraphs with line breaks. I use bold sparingly and for emphasis, not structure. For proposals: short, confident, no fluff. One page if possible.

## What I Dislike

Corporate voice. Anything that sounds like it was written by committee or reviewed by legal. "We are pleased to present" — just present it. "Utilize" instead of "use." "In order to" instead of "to." AI writing that's clearly AI writing — the telltale signs are hedging ("it's worth noting that"), list overuse, and the word "delve." I also hate when drafts are longer than they need to be. Say it in fewer words.

## By Context

With clients: warm, professional, direct. I want them to feel like they're working with a person, not a vendor. I use their first names, I'm honest about timelines, and I'll push back on bad ideas respectfully but clearly. On LinkedIn: more performative — I'm writing for an audience, so I'm punchier, more opinionated, and I use storytelling. In proposals: confident and brief. No "we would be delighted to..." — just "Here's what I'll do, here's what it costs, here's the timeline."

## Signature Patterns

I start LinkedIn posts with a bold claim or a short story. I say "here's the thing" a lot. I use "actually" for emphasis when correcting a common assumption. I end pieces with a single-sentence kicker. I never use emoji in professional writing. I never say "leverage," "synergy," "deep dive," or "at the end of the day."
